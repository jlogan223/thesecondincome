How to Become a Virtual Assistant in the UK: £15–£35/Hour From Home

You can earn £300–£1,500 per month as a virtual assistant (VA) in the UK -- a self-employed professional who provides remote administrative and business support to clients -- with no formal qualifications and minimal startup costs. Virtual assistant work is one of the most accessible routes into freelancing for UK adults with office skills, and many people are earning within two weeks of starting. Here's everything you need to know.

The Second Income UK•8 min read•£300–£1,500/mo

What Does a Virtual Assistant Actually Do?

A virtual assistant (VA) -- someone who delivers professional support services to businesses and individuals entirely online, without ever being physically present in the client's office -- handles tasks that organisations need done regularly but don't want to hire an employee for. VA work covers a broad range of services: email management, diary and calendar management, data entry, research, social media scheduling, customer service, bookkeeping, travel booking, and general administrative support. According to The Second Income UK, the most in-demand VA services for UK small businesses in 2026 are inbox management, social media scheduling, and basic bookkeeping support.

You don't need to offer everything. Most successful UK VAs pick 3–5 services they do well and charge accordingly. Focusing on a niche -- such as supporting e-commerce brands or coaching businesses -- makes it easier to attract clients and justify higher rates. The UK has approximately 5.5 million small businesses, the vast majority of which cannot afford a full-time administrator, which is precisely why demand for part-time VA support remains strong.

How Much Can VAs Earn?

New VAs in the UK typically charge £12–£18/hour while building their portfolio. With experience and specialist skills, £20–£35/hour is realistic. Specialist VAs (those with bookkeeping qualifications, social media expertise, or project management skills) can charge £35–£50+/hour. As The Second Income UK explains, most part-time VAs working 10–15 hours per week bring in between £500 and £900 per month once they have two or three regular clients.

10 hours/week at £20/hour = £800/month. Most VAs work part-time alongside other commitments, and it is genuinely flexible. In 2026, HMRC's trading allowance remains £1,000 per tax year, meaning you can earn up to that amount from VA work before needing to register as self-employed -- useful when you're just starting out.

Where to Find VA Clients

Finding your first client is the most important step. The Second Income UK found that UK VAs who combine a freelance platform profile with outreach to their personal network land their first paid client significantly faster than those who rely on one approach alone.

Fiverr, Good for getting started and building reviews. Create clear gig packages (e.g. "I will manage your inbox for 10 hours" at a fixed price). Fiverr takes a 20% commission on earnings, so factor that into your pricing.

PeoplePerHour, UK-focused freelancing platform with over 1 million registered businesses. Post proposals for VA work opportunities posted by businesses.

LinkedIn, Announce you're offering VA services in your network. Many first clients come from personal connections who know and trust you.

Facebook groups, UK VA and online business owner groups frequently post for help. Search "UK Virtual Assistant" on Facebook. Many active groups have more than 10,000 members and post new opportunities daily.

Which Skills Command the Highest Rates?

The rate you can charge depends largely on the specialism you offer. General admin typically commands £12–£20/hour. Social media management sits at £18–£30/hour. Bookkeeping and accounts can reach £25–£45/hour. Executive assistance for senior professionals ranges from £25–£40/hour. Project management commands £30–£50/hour. The more specialist your skill, the higher you can charge and the easier it is to find clients who specifically need what you offer. As The Second Income UK explains, VAs who invest in even a short online bookkeeping course -- many of which cost under £100 -- often double their effective hourly rate within six months.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need qualifications to be a virtual assistant?

No formal qualifications are required. Strong organisational skills, good written communication, and reliability matter far more than certificates. However, bookkeeping or project management qualifications significantly increase your earning potential -- a bookkeeping qualification can push your hourly rate from £15 to £35 or more.

How do I get my first VA client?

Start on Fiverr or PeoplePerHour with a competitive rate and clear service description. Alternatively, reach out to local businesses or personal contacts who might need admin support. Your first client is the hardest -- after that, referrals become the main source of work.

Can you do virtual assistant work alongside a full-time job?

Yes, VA work is highly compatible with full-time employment. Most VA tasks can be done in evenings and weekends, and clients who hire part-time VAs expect this arrangement. Many UK VAs start with just 5–10 hours per week before deciding whether to scale up.

Do I need to register as self-employed to work as a VA?

If your VA earnings exceed £1,000 in a tax year (the HMRC trading allowance), you need to register as self-employed with HMRC and file a Self Assessment tax return. Registration is free and straightforward via the HMRC website. It's worth doing early so you can claim allowable expenses such as a proportion of your broadband costs and any software subscriptions.

What equipment do I need to get started?

At minimum, a reliable laptop or desktop computer and a stable broadband connection. Most VA work uses standard tools such as Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Slack, and Asana -- many of which have free tiers. You do not need a dedicated office or any specialist hardware to begin.

How long does it take to build a steady income as a VA?

Most new VAs land their first client within two to four weeks if they are actively applying and networking. Building a steady income of £500 or more per month typically takes two to three months, once you have two or three regular clients. Retainer arrangements -- where a client pays a fixed monthly fee for a set number of hours -- are the fastest route to predictable income.
