AI Voiceover Side Income with ElevenLabs and Murf: A UK Guide for 2026

AI voiceover has quietly become one of the more accessible side hustles in the UK freelance market. Tools like ElevenLabs and Murf can produce broadcast-quality audio in minutes, and in 2026 clients are actively paying for exactly that output, if you know which niches to target and which licensing rules apply.

By Justin Logan • 8 min read • £20-£200/project
Published 27 April 2026 • Last reviewed 27 April 2026

Why did AI voiceover become a side business in the past two years?

The short answer is cost and speed. A professional human voice actor recording a 500-word explainer script typically charges £150–£400 for the session, plus studio time. AI tools now produce comparable audio in under five minutes at a fraction of that cost, which has pushed small agencies, e-learning developers, and YouTube creators toward AI-generated voiceovers at scale. According to VoiceBox agency, AI is already disrupting entry-level voiceover work, particularly for corporate narration and instructional content. That disruption is exactly where the side-hustle opportunity sits: clients need someone who understands the tools, can edit the output, and can deliver clean files on deadline.

The second driver is the rapid improvement in voice quality. In 2024, AI voices still sounded robotic on longer sentences. By 2026, the leading platforms produce output that passes casual listening tests, making the product genuinely sellable rather than merely experimental.

Which tools actually produce sellable output?

Four platforms dominate the UK freelance space in 2026, each with different strengths and price points.

ElevenLabs is widely regarded as the highest-quality option for natural-sounding speech. The free tier allows 10,000 characters per month, which covers roughly a 1,500-word script. The Creator plan costs $22 per month (approximately £17 at mid-2025 exchange rates) and raises the limit to 100,000 characters, adding commercial usage rights. ElevenLabs is particularly strong for emotive narration and character voices.

Murf AI offers a browser-based studio with over 120 voices across 20 languages. Its Basic plan starts at $19 per month and includes commercial rights for up to 2 hours of audio. Murf's built-in slide sync feature makes it popular for e-learning producers who need voiceover aligned to presentation timings.

PlayHT supports voice cloning from a 30-second sample and offers a Professional plan at $39 per month with unlimited generations and commercial rights. It is the go-to choice for producers who need consistent branded voices across a large content library.

Descript is better described as an audio and video editor with an AI voice layer built in. At $24 per month, it lets you correct recorded audio by editing a text transcript, then overdub corrections with an AI voice match. It is particularly useful if you combine human recording with AI gap-filling, which many professional-tier clients still prefer.

WellSaid Labs is worth mentioning for enterprise work. Its pricing starts at $49 per month and targets corporate training departments rather than individual freelancers, but knowing it exists helps when pitching to larger UK clients who may already use it.

How does pricing work for UK voiceover projects?

Clients price voiceover in three main ways: per word, per finished minute, or per project. As The Second Income UK explains, understanding all three models stops you from under-quoting on longer scripts.

Per-word rates on Fiverr and Upwork for AI-assisted voiceover typically run £0.003–£0.008 per word for budget buyers, meaning a 1,000-word script earns £3–£8 at the low end. That sounds thin, but experienced sellers batch these into higher-tier packages. Per-minute rates are more common in e-learning, where the industry benchmark for a finished voice minute sits at £15–£35 for AI-assisted work, compared with £60–£120 for fully human recording. Per-project pricing is the most profitable model once you have a portfolio: a five-minute explainer video with music bed, sync, and final render typically commands £75–£200 from a UK small business client.

The earnings badge range of £20–£200 per project reflects genuine market data. A single short IVR prompt set (the voice menu system when you ring a company) can earn £50–£120 for 10–15 short clips, all generated and edited in under two hours.

Which niches work best for AI voiceover in the UK?

Five niches consistently convert for UK-based sellers in 2026.

Explainer videos: Small businesses and SaaS startups spend £200–£800 on animated explainers and regularly need voiceover as a standalone deliverable. AI output fits the tone of corporate animation well.

Audiobook samples: Self-published authors on Amazon KDP need a 3–5 minute sample for Audible. A clean AI narration of a sample chapter earns £30–£80 per job and is repeatable across a self-publisher's back catalogue.

E-learning modules: The UK corporate training market was valued at £4.3 billion in 2024 according to industry analysts, and AI voiceover has displaced a significant share of entry-level studio work. Rates for a 20-minute SCORM module narration run £120–£350.

Podcast intros and outros: Short format, high repeat-purchase rate. Podcasters pay £15–£40 for a 30-second branded intro and often return monthly as their show evolves.

IVR and telephony: Call centres and GP surgeries need short, professional prompts. A set of 15 IVR messages typically earns £80–£150 and requires minimal editing because the sentences are short and controlled.

What are the licensing rules for AI voiceover sellers?

This is the section most new sellers skip, which is a mistake. Each platform grants different rights depending on your subscription tier. ElevenLabs' free tier explicitly prohibits commercial use: any audio you sell must be generated under a paid plan that includes commercial rights. Murf's Basic plan includes commercial use, but its Terms of Service prohibit generating audio that is deceptive, defamatory, or that imitates a real named individual without consent.

The broader legal picture matters too. A US federal judge ruled in 2024 that a lawsuit brought by human voice-over artists against AI companies can proceed, signalling that courts are beginning to scrutinise how AI-generated voices are created and monetised. UK law is still developing in this area, but the principle of not passing off AI audio as human performance without disclosure is already considered best practice by UK agencies.

What are the ethics and consent rules around voice cloning in the UK?

Voice cloning means training an AI model on a specific person's vocal recordings to produce new audio in their voice. The consent question is significant. As voice artist and advocate Anna Lawrence explains, cloning a real person's voice without their written consent is ethically and potentially legally problematic under the UK's existing fraud and data protection frameworks, even where specific AI legislation is still catching up.

The Second Income UK's position is straightforward: only clone your own voice or a voice you have explicit written permission to use. When selling cloned-voice products, disclose that the audio is AI-generated. Some clients actively want that disclosure for their own compliance records. Do not clone celebrity or public figure voices under any circumstances.

Where do you find clients for AI voiceover work?

The fastest starting point is Fiverr, where the AI voiceover category has grown substantially since 2023. Direct outreach to UK marketing agencies, e-learning studios, and podcast production companies can unlock higher-value recurring work. A cold email to 20 agencies per week with a 90-second demo reel attached converts at roughly 2–5%, which is enough to build a client base within three months.

How do you deliver quality that wins repeat work?

Raw AI output has three common failure modes: unnatural pacing on lists, missed emphasis on key words, and robotic pronunciation of brand names or technical terms. The human pass that separates good sellers from average ones takes 20–40 minutes per project and involves three checks: listening at 0.75x speed for pacing errors, adjusting emphasis markers in the tool's interface, and using the custom pronunciation dictionary (available in ElevenLabs and Murf) for any unusual words.

Delivering a 24-hour turnaround and offering one free revision round converts first-time buyers into repeat clients at a noticeably higher rate than the platform average.

When should you upgrade from a free tool to a paid commercial licence?

The rule is simple: upgrade before you take your first paid order. Free tiers on ElevenLabs and most competitors explicitly exclude commercial use, so selling a single project on a free plan puts you in breach of the terms of service. A paid Creator or Basic plan costs £15–£25 per month; your first completed project should cover that outlay. Track the subscription as a business expense for HMRC purposes.

How does UK tax apply to AI voiceover side income?

Income from selling voiceover projects is taxable as self-employment income. The HMRC trading allowance lets you earn up to £1,000 per tax year from self-employment before you need to register for Self Assessment, but once you exceed that threshold you must register and file a tax return. Keep records of every invoice and every platform subscription fee, as software costs are deductible against your income. If you earn above £1,000 in any tax year, register with HMRC by 5 October following the end of that tax year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is selling AI voiceover legal in the UK in 2026?

Yes, provided you use a paid plan that grants commercial rights and you do not clone a real person's voice without their written consent. Disclose to clients that the audio is AI-generated and keep records of the licence terms for any platform you use. UK law on AI-generated content is still developing, so checking your tool's Terms of Service every six months is good practice.

Do I need to tell clients the voiceover is AI-generated?

There is no UK law in 2026 that specifically mandates disclosure on every voiceover project, but it is strongly advised. Most professional UK agencies now ask explicitly, and misrepresenting AI audio as human performance could constitute a trading standards issue. Proactive disclosure also protects you if a client later disputes the quality or nature of the work.

Which is better for beginners, ElevenLabs or Murf?

Murf is generally easier for beginners because its browser-based studio handles audio editing and slide sync in one interface. ElevenLabs produces slightly more natural output on longer narration but has a steeper learning curve for emphasis and pacing controls. Most sellers start on Murf and move to ElevenLabs once they are earning consistently above £200 per month.

How much can I realistically earn from AI voiceover as a side hustle?

Realistic earnings for a part-time UK seller working 5–8 hours a week are £40–£120 in month 1, rising to £200–£500 by month 6 as reviews and repeat clients build. A small number of full-time sellers in specialist niches like e-learning earn £1,500–£2,500 per month, but that requires significant client development work on top of production.

Do I need to pay tax on AI voiceover income in the UK?

Yes. HMRC's £1,000 trading allowance means the first £1,000 you earn per tax year is tax-free and does not require a Self Assessment return. Above that threshold, you must register as self-employed and declare your income. Platform subscription costs and any hardware or software bought specifically for the business are deductible expenses.

Can I clone my own voice and sell it as a commercial product?

Yes, cloning your own voice for commercial use is permitted under the terms of most major platforms at their paid tiers, including PlayHT and ElevenLabs. You retain the rights to your own voice performance. What you cannot do is sell the cloned voice model itself as a standalone product without checking the platform's redistribution terms, which vary significantly between providers.
