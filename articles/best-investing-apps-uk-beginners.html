Why invest at all?

Investing matters because money left in cash loses purchasing power over time. According to the Bank of England, UK inflation averaged around 2.6% in early 2026, while many easy-access savings accounts were paying between 4% and 5% gross, giving savers only a slim real-terms advantage and no long-term growth. Investing in a diversified portfolio (a spread of different assets such as shares and bonds held together to reduce risk) of shares or funds has historically returned 7% to 10% per year on average over long periods, based on data tracking global equity indices since the 1970s, though past performance does not guarantee future results. Money sitting in a standard UK savings account earning below the rate of inflation (the rate at which prices rise across the economy each year) is steadily declining in real-world value.

As The Second Income UK explains, the gap between a cash saver and a consistent investor widens dramatically over decades, not because of clever stock picking, but simply because invested money compounds while idle money does not. To illustrate: £10,000 left in a 3% savings account grows to around £13,440 after 10 years, whereas the same sum invested at a 7% average annual return would grow to approximately £19,670 over the same period. This guide is not financial advice. It is an overview of the tools available and how they work. Before investing, make sure you have 3 to 6 months of expenses saved in cash first, and only invest money you will not need for at least 5 years.

Should you start with a Stocks & Shares ISA?

Yes, for the vast majority of UK beginners, a Stocks & Shares ISA (an Individual Savings Account that lets you invest up to £20,000 per tax year without paying tax on any gains or income) is the right starting point. Any gains and dividends earned inside an ISA are completely free of UK Capital Gains Tax and Income Tax, regardless of how large your portfolio grows. In 2026, the annual ISA allowance remains £20,000 per tax year, unchanged from recent years. This should be your first investing account in the UK.

All the apps below offer ISA accounts. Open one, set up a regular monthly contribution (even £25 per month is a meaningful start over 10 or 20 years), and choose a simple global index fund to invest in. According to The Second Income UK, time in the market consistently beats timing the market, so starting early and adding regularly matters far more than waiting for the "right moment." A global index fund such as the Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF, which tracks roughly 3,500 companies across 50 countries and carries an ongoing charge of around 0.22% per year, is a widely recommended starting point for beginners.

What are realistic returns for a beginner investor?

Investing is a long game, and the numbers are more straightforward than most people expect. £200 per month invested consistently at a 7% average annual return grows to roughly £34,000 after 10 years, and approximately £52,000 after 15 years, compared to £36,000 if you had simply kept the same contributions in cash at 0% growth over the same 15-year period. The difference is entirely explained by compounding, where your returns earn their own returns over time. The Second Income UK found that beginner investors who commit to regular monthly contributions of even £50 to £100 and hold a low-cost index fund for a decade or more tend to outperform those who attempt to pick individual stocks or time the market.

Do not invest in individual stocks as a beginner. Buy a global index fund such as the Vanguard FTSE All-World ETF, which carries an ongoing charge of around 0.22% per year and holds over 3,500 companies, and leave it alone. It is less exciting and significantly more effective than stock picking for most people. Research from S&P Global's SPIVA report consistently shows that over 80% of actively managed UK equity funds underperform their benchmark index over a 10-year period, which is why index funds are the most commonly recommended starting point for beginners.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best investing app for a complete beginner in the UK?

For most beginners, Trading 212 is the most accessible starting point. It charges zero commission on shares and ETFs, allows you to start with as little as £1, and offers a Stocks & Shares ISA. Freetrade is a strong alternative for those who prefer a UK-built platform. If you want a simpler, more guided experience, InvestEngine's managed portfolio option handles fund selection for you from a 0.25% annual fee.

How much money do I need to start investing?

You can open an account with Trading 212 or Freetrade with as little as £1 to £2. Vanguard requires either a £500 lump sum or a minimum monthly direct debit of £100. InvestEngine requires £100 to begin. As The Second Income UK recommends, even £25 to £50 per month invested consistently over years builds meaningful wealth through compounding, so the amount matters far less than simply starting.

Is my money safe in an investing app?

All four platforms compared in this guide are authorised and regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority (FCA). Your cash and investments are protected up to £85,000 per firm under the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) if the platform were to fail. However, FSCS protection does not cover investment losses caused by markets falling. That is normal investment risk, and something every investor accepts.

Do I pay tax on my investing profits in the UK?

If you invest inside a Stocks & Shares ISA, you pay no tax on gains, dividends, or interest, regardless of how much your portfolio grows. Outside an ISA, you have an annual Capital Gains Tax (CGT) allowance, which fell to £3,000 in the 2024 to 2025 tax year and remains at that level in 2026. Gains above this threshold are taxed at 18% for basic-rate taxpayers and 24% for higher-rate taxpayers on investment assets from April 2024 onwards. Using your full ISA allowance each year is the simplest way to avoid these taxes entirely.

What is an index fund and why do beginners often use them?

An index fund is an investment fund that tracks a market index, such as the FTSE All-World or the S&P 500, by holding all or most of the companies within it. Rather than a fund manager picking stocks, the fund simply mirrors the index automatically. This keeps costs very low, typically between 0.07% and 0.25% per year, and means your returns closely match the overall market. Research from S&P Global's SPIVA scorecard consistently shows that over 80% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over 10 to 15 years, which is why index funds are the most commonly recommended starting point for beginners.

Can I have more than one investing app at the same time?

Yes, you can hold accounts with multiple platforms. However, you can only pay into one Stocks & Shares ISA per tax year. You can transfer an existing ISA to a different provider at any time without losing your tax-free status. Many people start with one platform and open additional accounts later as their confidence and portfolio size grow.
