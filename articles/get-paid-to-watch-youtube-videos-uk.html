Get Paid to Watch YouTube Videos in the UK in 2026

Yes, you can get paid to watch YouTube videos and other online clips in the UK in 2026, but the earnings are modest. Platforms like Swagbucks and InboxPounds pay real rewards, typically between &pound;5 and &pound;30 a month for regular use, and this guide covers exactly which sites are worth your time, how payouts work, and how to avoid the scams.

By Justin Logan • 7 min read • £5–£30/mo
Published 19 July 2026 • Last reviewed 19 July 2026

Does anyone actually pay you to watch YouTube videos in the UK?

Yes, legitimate "get paid to" (GPT) platforms do pay UK users to watch videos, but it helps to understand what you are actually doing. These sites earn advertising revenue by serving you video ads or sponsored content, then share a small slice of that revenue with you in the form of points or cashback. You are not being paid to watch YouTube directly on YouTube's own platform. Instead, you are watching short clips or adverts hosted within a rewards app, and some of those clips are YouTube-hosted videos embedded within the platform.

According to The Second Income UK, the honest earning potential sits between £5 and £30 per month for someone who uses two or three GPT platforms consistently. That is not a significant income, but it costs nothing to join and the barrier to entry is zero. The key is treating it as background micro-earning rather than a primary hustle. As the r/beermoneyglobal community on Reddit regularly confirms, dedicated users rarely clear more than a few pounds per hour from video tasks alone.

Which legitimate platforms reward video watching in the UK?

Not every GPT site accepts UK users or offers video tasks. The following three are consistently available to UK residents and have a track record of paying out.

Swagbucks

Swagbucks is the largest rewards platform in the UK and one of the most established globally, having paid out over $700 million (roughly £550 million) in rewards since its launch. Watching videos earns you SB points, and the video section includes news clips, entertainment content and short adverts. You typically earn 1 to 3 SB per video playlist watched, with playlists running 3 to 5 minutes each. 100 SB converts to roughly £0.80, so the effective rate for video watching alone is low. Swagbucks pays out via PayPal cash or gift cards including Amazon, ASOS and Argos, with a minimum redemption of 300 SB (approximately £2.40).

InboxPounds

InboxPounds is a UK-based rewards site that pays in sterling rather than points, which makes it simpler to track your earnings. The video section includes news digests, celebrity content and short ad reels. According to LatestFreeStuff's InboxPounds guide, users earn roughly £0.01 to £0.02 per video clip watched, and the minimum withdrawal threshold is £10. Reaching that £10 threshold from video watching alone could take 10 to 15 hours, so combining video tasks with surveys and emails is the practical approach.

Freecash

Freecash is a newer platform available to UK users that pays in coins redeemable for PayPal cash, Bitcoin or gift cards. Its video section works similarly to Swagbucks. Freecash's own explainer notes that video offers typically pay between $0.001 and $0.01 per video, with higher-paying sponsored videos available periodically. The minimum withdrawal on Freecash is $3 (around £2.40 at mid-2026 rates) via PayPal, which is lower than InboxPounds and makes it easier to see early payouts.

Rewarded Play and other apps

Rewarded Play is primarily a mobile gaming rewards app rather than a video platform, but it does include ad video tasks. Users earn points by watching short in-app video adverts between games. Points convert to Amazon vouchers, with most users reporting around £5 to £10 per month if they use the app daily. The European Funding Guide's 2026 round-up of UK video earning platforms lists several further options including PaidWork and Appen, though availability and task volume vary by region within the UK.

How much can you realistically earn per hour and per month?

The Second Income UK found that, across Swagbucks, InboxPounds and Freecash combined, active UK users report earnings of between £0.30 and £0.60 per hour spent on video tasks. That is significantly below the 2026 National Living Wage of £12.21 per hour, so framing this as a primary income source would be misleading.

On a monthly basis, users who spend around 30 minutes per day across platforms and include other task types (surveys, offers, search) typically reach £15 to £30 per month. Users relying on video tasks only, spending 20 minutes per day, are more likely to earn £5 to £10 per month. According to PaidWork's guide to earning from video watching, the platforms with the highest per-video rates tend to be those offering sponsored brand surveys embedded within video content, rather than passive ad-watching playlists.

Tax and HMRC rules for micro-earning income

UK residents have a trading allowance of £1,000 per tax year, which means you can earn up to £1,000 gross from self-employment or miscellaneous income sources without paying Income Tax on it or needing to file a Self Assessment return solely for that income. Realistically, earning £30 per month from GPT platforms means you would earn no more than £360 per year, well within the £1,000 trading allowance.

However, if you combine GPT earnings with other side hustle income such as selling on eBay, completing surveys or freelancing, the total could exceed £1,000. In that case you would need to register for Self Assessment and declare the combined income. HMRC treats cashback and gift card rewards from GPT platforms as miscellaneous income, not as gifts. As The Second Income UK explains, keeping a simple spreadsheet of your monthly earnings across all platforms is the sensible approach so that you can check your annual total against the £1,000 threshold at the end of each tax year.

Avoiding scams that promise unrealistic pay for watching videos

The phrase "get paid to watch videos" attracts a significant number of fraudulent sites. As The Second Income UK found when researching this topic, several sites advertise earnings of £50 to £200 per hour for watching videos. That figure is not achievable on any legitimate platform. Red flags to look for include:

  • No verifiable company registration or physical UK address
  • Claims of earning £1 or more per video clip watched
  • Requiring an upfront payment or "activation fee" to access tasks
  • Payout methods that are only cryptocurrency with no PayPal or gift card option
  • No independent reviews on Trustpilot or similar verification sites

Legitimate platforms like Swagbucks and InboxPounds are listed on the UK Information Commissioner's Office register and have hundreds of thousands of verifiable user reviews. If a site you have not heard of is promising £10 per hour just for watching YouTube clips, treat that as a scam.

Tips to maximise your earnings without wasting time

Video watching pays best when you treat it as a background activity rather than a focused task. Here are practical steps to make the most of the limited earnings available:

  • Stack platforms: Running Swagbucks on one tab and InboxPounds on another while you work or relax effectively doubles your passive accumulation without doubling your attention.
  • Complete sign-up bonuses: Swagbucks regularly offers 300 to 500 SB (roughly £2.40 to £4) for new sign-ups who complete a first task within 30 days. InboxPounds typically pays a £1 welcome bonus.
  • Combine video with surveys: On both Swagbucks and InboxPounds, doing a 10-minute survey (£0.50 to £1.50) while a video playlist auto-plays in another tab is the highest-yield use of your time on these platforms.
  • Watch on mobile: The Swagbucks app and InboxPounds app let you watch video playlists while commuting or in a waiting room. Users who do this report an extra 50 to 100 SB per week (approximately £0.40 to £0.80) without any additional screen time at home.
  • Redeem little and often: Platforms with low minimum thresholds like Freecash (£2.40 minimum) let you cash out frequently, which is psychologically motivating and reduces the risk of a platform closing before you withdraw.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you get paid to watch YouTube videos in the UK in 2026?

You cannot earn money directly on YouTube as a viewer. However, UK-based GPT platforms including Swagbucks, InboxPounds and Freecash pay you to watch short video clips, many of which are YouTube-hosted, within their own apps. Realistic earnings are between £5 and £30 per month across multiple platforms.

How much does Swagbucks pay per video watched in the UK?

Swagbucks typically pays 1 to 3 SB per playlist of 3 to 5 videos. As 100 SB equals roughly £0.80, each playlist earns you approximately £0.008 to £0.024. The rate is low, which is why video watching is best combined with higher-paying tasks like surveys.

Do I need to pay tax on earnings from GPT platforms?

If your total miscellaneous and trading income from all side hustles stays below £1,000 in a tax year, the UK trading allowance means no tax is owed and no Self Assessment filing is required. If you earn more than £1,000 across all sources combined, you must register with HMRC and declare it.

What is the minimum amount I need to earn before I can cash out on InboxPounds?

InboxPounds requires a minimum balance of £10 before you can request a withdrawal. You can cash out via PayPal, cheque or gift cards. Reaching £10 from video tasks alone typically takes 10 to 15 hours, so combining videos with survey and email tasks speeds up the process.

Are there scam sites pretending to pay for watching YouTube?

Yes. Sites claiming to pay £1 or more per video, or £50 per hour for watching content, are not legitimate. Genuine platforms pay fractions of a penny per video. Check for a Trustpilot profile, a verifiable company address and established PayPal withdrawal options before spending any time on a new site.

What earns more: watching videos or completing surveys online?

Paid surveys earn significantly more per hour. A 10-minute survey on a reputable UK site typically pays £0.50 to £1.50, versus around £0.01 to £0.02 per video clip. If you have limited spare time, surveys are the better use of it, with video watching best treated as a passive background top-up.
