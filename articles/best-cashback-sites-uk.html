Best Cashback Sites in the UK for 2026

The cashback sites UK shoppers actually earn from in 2026, ranked honestly. Fees, payout reliability, and how to stack cashback with credit card rewards.

The Second Income UK • 9 min read • £200-£800/yr

Cashback sites are one of the few genuinely passive ways to save money. You shop through a portal, a retailer pays a referral fee, and the site passes most of it back to you. There is no survey to fill in, no app to fiddle with and no skill required. You just click before you buy.

The catch is that not all cashback sites are equally reliable, equally generous, or equally honest about their fees. This guide covers the main players available to UK shoppers in 2026, what you can realistically expect to earn, and how to combine cashback with other rewards for maximum return.

What Cashback Sites Actually Pay

Cashback rates are set by retailers, not by the cashback site itself. A site has no control over whether a retailer pays 1% or 10%. What the site does control is the share it keeps for itself. Most of the big platforms take somewhere between 10% and 40% of the retailer's referral fee before passing the rest to you. That slice covers their running costs, marketing and profit margin.

Rates also vary by category. Financial products such as insurance and credit cards tend to pay the highest cashback, sometimes £30 to £80 for a single purchase. Supermarkets and grocery delivery services sit at the low end, often 0.5% to 2%. Electronics and fashion land somewhere in the middle.

One thing worth knowing: cashback is tracked via a cookie or redirect. If you clear your cookies, use a different browser, or go through a comparison site first, the tracking can fail and you may not receive your cashback. This happens more often than it should, so it is worth checking your account a week or two after each purchase.

TopCashback in Detail

TopCashback is the largest cashback site in the UK by membership and consistently offers some of the highest rates. The free membership tier gives you access to nearly all retailers with no monthly fee. There is a paid "Plus" tier at around £5 per month (or roughly £50 per year) which promises slightly higher rates on selected retailers, but for most casual shoppers the free tier is perfectly adequate.

Payouts can be withdrawn to your bank account, PayPal, or as gift cards. Withdrawing as a gift card sometimes gets you a small bonus on top of your cashback balance, though the practical value of that depends on whether you actually want the card in question.

Cashback is usually tracked within a few days and confirmed once the retailer pays out, which can take anywhere from a few weeks to a few months depending on the retailer's return window. TopCashback holds the cashback until it is confirmed, so do not expect instant access to every penny you earn.

The breadth of retailers is strong, covering most major UK shops, insurance providers, broadband suppliers, travel companies and utilities.

Quidco in Detail

Quidco is TopCashback's closest rival and the two trade blows on rates depending on which retailer you are looking at. Quidco used to charge a £1 monthly fee on its free tier, deducted from your cashback earnings. As of 2026 the free membership has no monthly fee, though a premium tier at around £5 per month offers higher rates on selected retailers, similar to TopCashback's model.

One feature Quidco has that TopCashback does not is its ClickSnap feature, which lets you earn cashback on in-store purchases by photographing your receipt. The selection of participating retailers is relatively limited but it is a useful bonus.

Payouts work much the same way as TopCashback: bank transfer, PayPal, or gift cards. Cashback tracking and confirmation timescales are broadly similar too.

The honest answer is that both platforms are genuinely good and the best strategy is to check both before any significant purchase and use whichever is offering the higher rate that day. For a proper breakdown, take a look at our head-to-head TopCashback vs Quidco comparison.

Other UK Cashback Sites Worth Knowing About

Airtime Rewards

Airtime Rewards links to your debit or credit card and automatically earns cashback when you spend in-store or online at participating retailers. You do not need to click a portal or remember to activate anything. The cashback goes towards your mobile phone bill, which is a slightly unusual but perfectly decent payout method if you are not on a fixed contract.

The retailer selection is narrower than TopCashback or Quidco, focused mainly on high street names, but the automatic nature of it means you will almost never miss out due to forgotten clicks. It works well as a complement to the main platforms rather than a replacement.

Jam Doughnut

Jam Doughnut is a newer entrant that focuses on offering higher rates than the big two on certain retailers by taking a smaller cut for itself. It is worth adding to your routine check before any big purchase, though the retailer list is still shorter than the established players. The platform is straightforward to use and has received broadly positive feedback from early users.

Nectar

Nectar is not a cashback site in the traditional sense but Sainsbury's, Argos, eBay and a range of other retailers all feed into the Nectar point system, and Nectar points can be converted into real spending value at the checkout. If you already shop at Sainsbury's regularly it is worth linking your Nectar account to their cashback portal to earn extra points on top of your standard loyalty points. The effective cashback rate is modest (typically 0.5% to 1%), but it stacks on top of everything else.

How to Stack Cashback with Credit Card Rewards

This is where the returns start to add up properly. Most cashback credit cards and rewards credit cards do not prohibit you from also using a cashback portal. So the flow looks like this: you click through TopCashback, make your purchase using an American Express cashback card or a similar rewards card, and you earn on both simultaneously.

A practical example: say you spend £500 on a new laptop. TopCashback might offer 2% cashback through the retailer, giving you £10. Your rewards credit card earns 0.5% cashback, giving you £2.50. That is £12.50 back on a single purchase for about thirty seconds of extra effort.

The important rules to follow here are: always pay your credit card off in full each month (otherwise the interest will wipe out any reward), and always click the cashback portal before going to the retailer's site. Do not add items to your basket before activating the portal link, as this can break tracking.

Some people also stack on top of in-store loyalty cards where applicable. Boots Advantage Card, for instance, earns points on purchases that can also be tracked through a cashback portal for the online store.

Realistic Yearly Earnings

Your earnings depend almost entirely on how much you spend through the portals and on what. Someone who remembers to click through for a broadband renewal, a holiday booking, some insurance renewals, a few clothing orders and their Christmas shopping could reasonably accumulate £200 to £400 a year on the free tier of TopCashback or Quidco without changing their spending habits at all.

People who are more systematic about it, checking both platforms before every purchase and stacking with credit card rewards, tend to report £400 to £800 per year. That figure is achievable but it does require consistent effort and it assumes a reasonably active household budget going through the portals.

The £800 end of the range typically involves one or two large cashback events in a year, such as switching energy supplier, taking out a new insurance policy, or booking a significant holiday through a portal that pays well. These individual transactions can earn £30 to £100 each.

What you should not expect is passive income that keeps growing without any input. If you stop clicking, you stop earning.

Tax Treatment of Cashback

For the vast majority of people, cashback earnings are not taxable. HMRC generally treats cashback as a discount on a purchase rather than income. If you earn £500 a year from cashback sites as an individual consumer spending your own money, you almost certainly do not need to declare it.

The situation can be different if you are earning cashback on business purchases and claiming those purchases as expenses, or if you have set up some kind of cashback arbitrage scheme at scale. In those cases it is worth checking the specifics. Our HMRC side hustle tax guide covers the broader picture of when HMRC takes an interest in extra earnings.

For everyday household use, cashback is simply a saving, not income, and you do not need to worry about it from a tax perspective.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it safe to use cashback sites?

Yes, the established platforms (TopCashback, Quidco) have been operating for well over a decade and have paid out hundreds of millions of pounds to UK members. You are not giving them your payment details directly. They simply redirect you to the retailer's site via a tracked link. The main risk is that a transaction fails to track, in which case you can raise a claim through the cashback site.

Why does my cashback sometimes show as pending for months?

Retailers do not pay out until they are confident the sale is final, meaning no refund has been requested. A retailer with a 60-day return policy will typically hold off paying the cashback site for at least that long. Once the retailer pays the cashback site, the site then releases the cashback to your account. It is not instant, and it can feel frustrating, but it is how the whole system works.

Can I use both TopCashback and Quidco at the same time?

Not on a single purchase. You can only click through one portal per transaction, as using two would cause tracking conflicts and likely result in neither paying out. What you can do is check both platforms before you buy and use whichever is offering the better rate for that specific retailer at that moment.

Do cashback sites work on mobile?

They do, but you need to be careful. Using the cashback site's own app or clicking through from the mobile browser tends to work better than clicking from a desktop and then switching devices. In-app browsers on social media platforms can break tracking. Stick to Safari or Chrome on mobile, and check the retailer on the cashback site's mobile app before heading to the retailer's site.

Are cashback rates consistent throughout the year?

No, they fluctuate regularly. Retailers increase rates during promotions and drop them at other times. Black Friday, January sales, and the run-up to Christmas often see elevated rates. Signing up for email alerts from your cashback platform means you will be notified when rates go up on retailers you use regularly.

What happens if a retailer goes bust while my cashback is pending?

If the retailer fails before paying the cashback platform, the platform typically cannot pay you either. In this scenario you would lose that pending cashback. It is an unlikely outcome with major retailers but worth knowing about. The cashback platforms generally try to chase outstanding payments even in these circumstances, though they cannot guarantee recovery.
