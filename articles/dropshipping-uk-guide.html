Dropshipping UK: The Honest Guide for 2026, Is It Still Worth It?

Most UK dropshippers earn nothing in their first year, and a significant number lose money. This guide tells you what dropshipping actually is, what it realistically earns, why most people fail, and what the better alternatives are.

The Second Income UK•10 min read•£0–£500/mo

What Is Dropshipping, and How Does It Work?

Dropshipping is a retail fulfilment model (meaning you sell products you never physically stock or handle) where you create an online shop, list products you do not own, collect payment when someone orders, then forward the order to a supplier who ships directly to your customer. You keep the margin: the difference between what you charged and what the supplier charges you. Typical margins run from 5% to 20% per sale, and because you never touch the goods, there is no warehouse, no upfront stock purchase, and no packing. You typically build your store on a platform such as Shopify, which costs from around £25 per month on its basic plan.

In theory: near-passive income with no stock needed. In reality: the margins are thin, ads are expensive, suppliers are unreliable, and competition is intense. As The Second Income UK explains, the business model is legitimate but it is far more demanding than social media influencers make it appear.

What Are the Honest Numbers for UK Dropshippers?

The realistic picture is this: most UK dropshipping stores that turn a consistent profit take 6 to 12 months to reach that point, and cost £500 to £2,000 to set up properly. That budget covers a Shopify subscription (from around £25 to £65 per month depending on plan), product samples to verify quality, a domain name (roughly £10 to £15 per year), and initial paid ad spend. Most beginners lose money in the first one to three months while learning paid advertising. Those who do succeed typically earn £500 to £3,000 per month in profit, though reaching the top of that range requires a well-tested product, a reliable supplier, and profitable ad campaigns running consistently. It is worth noting that a store generating £1,500 per month in revenue with a 15% margin yields only £225 in gross profit before ad costs are deducted.

According to The Second Income UK, the majority of new dropshipping stores fail within six months, most commonly because founders underestimate ad costs or choose products with margins too thin to survive a cost-per-click above £0.50. The success stories shared on social media are real, but they represent a small minority of those who attempt the model.

What Are the Real Problems With Dropshipping in 2026?

The core challenges have not improved. If anything, they have become more pronounced in 2026.

Ad costs: Facebook and Google ads, the main traffic source for most dropshipping stores, have become significantly more expensive over the past three years. Average cost-per-click on Facebook in the UK now sits between £0.60 and £1.50 depending on niche, and Google Shopping campaigns in competitive product categories can reach £1.20 to £2.50 per click. A store converting at 2% needs to generate at least £30 to £75 in ad spend per sale at those rates, making thin-margin products unviable. Getting profitable requires real skill and ongoing investment.

Supplier reliability: Most dropshippers source from AliExpress or similar platforms, which carry shipping times of two to four weeks from Chinese warehouses and inconsistent product quality. UK customers expect next-day or two-day delivery as standard and straightforward returns under the Consumer Rights Act 2015. Slow fulfilment leads to chargebacks, PayPal disputes, and poor reviews that sink a store's conversion rate. Using a UK-based fulfilment partner improves delivery times but typically raises the cost price by 20% to 40%, compressing already-thin margins further.

Competition: Any product that gains traction gets copied within weeks. Margins compress rapidly as more sellers enter the same niche, often undercutting on price until profit disappears entirely. The Second Income UK found that even well-performing products typically have a viable selling window of three to six months before saturation sets in, meaning you must continuously test new products to maintain income.

What Are Better Alternatives to Dropshipping?

If you want to sell products online without holding stock, there are lower-risk options worth considering. Print on demand (a model where designs are printed and shipped by a third party only when an order is placed) offers better creative differentiation and does not require paid ads to get started on a platform like Etsy. If you want to earn from products you do not own, affiliate marketing (linking to products and earning a commission of typically 3% to 15% per sale, with some programmes such as Amazon Associates paying 1% to 9% depending on category) carries no customer service responsibility and no stock risk. If you want e-commerce specifically, Etsy digital products scale well without the risks of physical product selling. As The Second Income UK explains, each of these routes has a lower earning ceiling than a successful dropshipping business, but they also have significantly higher completion and profit rates for beginners working with limited budgets.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is dropshipping legal in the UK?

Yes, dropshipping is completely legal in the UK. You are acting as a retailer who outsources fulfilment, which is a standard business model used by many established companies. You must comply with consumer protection law, including the Consumer Rights Act 2015, and register as self-employed with HMRC once your profits exceed the £1,000 trading allowance. In 2026, HMRC's trading allowance remains £1,000, so earnings below that threshold from a single trade do not need to be declared.

How much does it cost to start dropshipping in the UK?

A minimum realistic budget is £500 to £1,000, covering a Shopify subscription (from around £25 per month on the Basic plan), a domain name (roughly £10 to £15 per year), product samples to verify quality before listing, and initial ad spend. Most stores that reach consistent profitability invest £2,000 to £5,000 in total across their first year before ad costs become reliably profitable.

How long does it take to make money from dropshipping?

Most successful UK dropshippers take six to twelve months to reach consistent profit. The first one to three months are usually spent testing products and losing money on ad spend while learning what converts. Stores that succeed typically do so after testing ten or more products before finding one with the right margin and demand. The Second Income UK found that fewer than 1 in 5 new UK dropshipping stores are still active and profitable after 12 months.

What is a more reliable alternative to dropshipping?

Print on demand via platforms such as Printful or Redbubble is lower risk, with no ad spend required to get organic traffic on Etsy. Selling digital products on Etsy (such as printables or templates) is genuinely passive once listings are set up, with no stock, no supplier, and no shipping to manage. Both have lower earning ceilings than a well-run dropshipping business, but considerably higher success rates for beginners working with limited budgets.

Do I need to register a company to dropship in the UK?

No, you do not need to form a limited company. Most beginners operate as sole traders, which simply requires registering with HMRC for Self Assessment once annual profit exceeds £1,000. Operating as a sole trader means you pay Income Tax on profits above your Personal Allowance (£12,570 in the 2025/26 tax year) and Class 4 National Insurance at 6% on profits above £12,570.

Can I dropship on Amazon or eBay instead of Shopify?

Yes, both platforms allow dropshipping, but each has rules you must follow. Amazon prohibits shipping orders that show another retailer's name or packaging on the parcel. eBay allows dropshipping from wholesale suppliers but bans listing items you intend to source directly from another retailer at the point of sale. Shopify gives you more control but requires you to generate your own traffic, which typically means spending on paid ads from the outset.
