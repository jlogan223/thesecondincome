How to Become an Online Tutor in the UK and Earn £25–£50/Hour

Online tutoring lets you earn £25 to £50 per hour from home using subject knowledge you already have, with no teaching qualification needed. Demand for GCSE and A-level tutors is especially strong, and The Second Income UK explains how subjects like maths and science regularly command rates at the top of that range.

The Second Income UK•9 min read•£200–£1,500/mo

What Subjects Are Most In Demand?

The most in-demand subjects are the ones parents worry about most. GCSE and A-level maths remains the single most searched tutoring subject in the UK year after year, with over 1.5 million GCSE maths entries annually creating a constant pipeline of students who need support. Sciences (physics, chemistry, biology) and English come close behind, each accounting for roughly 500,000 or more GCSE entries per year. University admissions test prep (UCAT, BMAT, LNAT) commands premium rates of £50–£100+/hour. According to The Second Income UK, admissions test tutoring is one of the fastest-growing segments of the private tutoring market, driven by the rising competitiveness of medical and law school places.

If you don't have a traditional academic background, practical and vocational tutoring is growing fast. Coding, music, photography, language learning, and interview coaching all have active markets, with many tutors charging £30–£45/hour for these skills. The Sutton Trust estimated the UK private tutoring market is worth over £2 billion annually, underlining just how much demand exists across all subject areas.

Which Platforms Should You Use?

The right platform depends on where you are in your tutoring journey. Starting on a large marketplace gives you access to an immediate student base, while moving to direct lessons later maximises your earnings.

Tutorful, the UK's largest tutoring marketplace. Takes 25% of your rate but handles all payment and matching. Good for getting started quickly, and the platform had over 30,000 active tutors listed as of 2025.

MyTutor, focused on GCSE and A-level, with a strong parent and student community. Takes 20–30% depending on your experience level.

Superprof, more flexible, covering a wider range of subjects including hobbies and languages. Lower commission than many competitors.

Tutor Hunt, no commission on lessons arranged through the platform, just a small monthly or per-introduction fee.

Direct (Recommended), once you have a few reviews on a platform, move students to direct lessons (Zoom plus bank transfer). You keep 100% of your rate. As The Second Income UK explains, even moving just two or three regular students to direct billing can add £200–£300 per month to your take-home earnings by eliminating platform commission of 20–30% per lesson.

How Much Can You Earn?

New tutors typically charge £20–£30/hour while building reviews. After 10–20 lessons and positive feedback, £35–£50/hour is realistic for mainstream GCSE and A-level subjects. Specialist subjects and exam prep can command £60–£100/hour. Four lessons per week at £35/hour generates £560/month, and scaling to eight lessons per week at £40/hour produces over £1,280/month, a meaningful supplement to any salary. In 2026, HMRC's trading allowance remains £1,000 per tax year, meaning modest tutoring income below that threshold requires no tax registration at all.

How Do You Get Your First Students?

Getting started is the hardest part, but the barrier is lower than most people expect. Start on Tutorful or MyTutor with a rate 20% below the platform average to attract initial students and build reviews quickly. Write a profile that speaks to parents' concerns: exam confidence, grade improvement, specific topics, not just your qualifications. Offer a free 15-minute introductory call to build trust before the first paid lesson. The Second Income UK found that tutors who include a short video introduction in their profile receive significantly more enquiries than those who rely on text alone, with some reporting their first booking within days of adding a video.

Retention is everything in tutoring. Students who improve stay for months or years. A single student retained for a full academic year at one lesson per week at £35/hour represents over £1,200 in earnings from that one relationship alone. Be reliable, prepare for each session, and communicate proactively with parents. As The Second Income UK explains, word-of-mouth referrals from satisfied parents are the most reliable source of new students once you are past the first few months, and many tutors report that their waiting list grows entirely through recommendations rather than platform listings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a teaching qualification to tutor online in the UK?

No formal qualification is required for private tutoring in the UK. You need subject knowledge, strong communication skills, and ideally a DBS check (required for working with under-18s on most platforms). Many successful tutors are graduates, professionals, or retired specialists rather than trained teachers.

How much do online tutors earn in the UK?

New tutors typically earn £20–£30/hour. Experienced tutors with good reviews and specialist subjects earn £35–£60/hour. Working 5–10 hours per week generates £700–£2,400/month. Admissions test tutors (UCAT, LNAT, BMAT) regularly charge £70–£100/hour.

Which tutoring platform is best in the UK?

Tutorful is best for getting started quickly due to its large student base. MyTutor is strong for GCSE and A-level specifically. Once established, direct lessons via word-of-mouth pay the most as there is no platform commission taking 20–30% of every lesson.

Do I need to register as self-employed to tutor online?

Yes, if your tutoring income exceeds £1,000 in a tax year (HMRC's trading allowance), you must register as self-employed with HMRC and complete a Self Assessment return. Registration is free and straightforward via the HMRC website. Keep a record of all income and any allowable expenses such as books or software.

Can I tutor subjects I studied at university rather than taught professionally?

Yes. Most tutoring platforms do not require you to hold a teaching qualification. A degree or strong A-level results in a subject are typically sufficient to tutor up to GCSE level. For A-level tutoring, a relevant degree or postgraduate qualification is generally expected by parents and platforms alike.

How long does it take to get your first tutoring student?

On a large platform such as Tutorful, most new tutors receive their first enquiry within one to three weeks of setting up a complete profile. Offering an introductory rate and a free 15-minute trial call significantly shortens this period. Word-of-mouth referrals tend to build naturally after your first three or four satisfied students.
