How this calculator works

The UK side hustle tax calculation has more moving parts than people expect. The headline figure depends on what else you earn, how you choose to deduct expenses, where you live in the UK, and whether you receive any dividends. This calculator handles each one in line with current HMRC rules for tax year 2026/27.

Step 1: Self-employment profit

Your gross side hustle income is reduced by either the £1,000 trading allowance OR your actual expenses, whichever gives the lower taxable profit. You cannot claim both. If your gross income is at or below £1,000, the full relief applies and your taxable profit is zero. (See the Trading Allowance Calculator for that side of the calculation in isolation.)

Step 2: Personal Allowance

The Personal Allowance is £12,570 for 2026/27, frozen at this level until April 2031. It applies identically to employment and self-employment income. For high earners with adjusted net income above £100,000, the allowance tapers down by £1 for every £2 of income over the threshold, reaching zero at £125,140. The calculator applies the taper automatically.

Step 3: Income Tax

Your day job salary and self-employment profit (combined non-savings, non-dividend income) is taxed at the standard rates after the Personal Allowance: 20% basic, 40% higher, 45% additional. Scottish taxpayers face six bands: 19% starter, 20% basic, 21% intermediate, 42% higher, 45% advanced, 48% top. The Scottish bands only apply to non-dividend income.

Step 4: Dividends

Dividends sit at the top of the income stack and use rUK band thresholds regardless of where you live. The first £500 of dividend income is covered by the dividend allowance (taxed at 0% but counts towards your band). Beyond that, dividend rates for 2026/27 are 10.75% ordinary, 35.75% upper, and 39.35% additional, all of which apply based on which band each portion of dividend income falls into.

Note: the ordinary and upper dividend rates each rose by 2 percentage points on 6 April 2026 (up from 8.75% and 33.75%). If you have a calculator from earlier years it will give the wrong answer.

Step 5: Class 4 National Insurance

Self-employment profit above the Lower Profits Limit (£12,570 for 2026/27) attracts Class 4 NI at 6%, up to the Upper Profits Limit of £50,270. Profit above that is charged at 2%. Class 4 is calculated on profit AFTER the trading allowance or expenses are deducted.

Class 2 NI

Mandatory Class 2 NI was effectively abolished from April 2024. If your self-employment profit is at or above the Small Profits Threshold of £7,105, you are treated as having paid Class 2 (your State Pension qualifying year is protected with no cash payment). Below £7,105, Class 2 is voluntary at £3.65 per week if you want to maintain your NI record.

What this calculator does NOT handle

  • Partnership income (sole traders only)
  • Pre-trading expenses and capital allowances
  • Pension contributions (which can reduce adjusted net income)
  • Student loan repayments
  • The High Income Child Benefit Charge
  • Marriage Allowance transfer
  • Property income (use the Property Allowance instead)
  • Income tax payments on account scheduling

For any of the above, speak to a qualified UK accountant or refer to HMRC's Self Assessment guidance.
