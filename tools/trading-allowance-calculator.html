What is the trading allowance?

The trading allowance is a tax exemption set by HMRC. For the 2026/27 tax year it is £1,000 of gross trading income per person, per tax year. It applies to most small-scale self-employment and casual income, including online selling, freelance work, content creation, ride-share driving, food delivery, hobbies that earn money, and most other "side hustle" activities.

If your gross income is at or below £1,000

You do not need to register for Self Assessment in respect of that income, and you do not owe Income Tax or Class 4 National Insurance on it. The whole lot is covered by the allowance.

There are a few exceptions where you might still want to register. The main ones: if you want to pay voluntary Class 2 NI to protect your State Pension record, if you want Tax-Free Childcare based on self-employment, or if you need to declare a loss to claim relief.

If your gross income is above £1,000

You have a choice on your Self Assessment return. You can either:

  • Claim the trading allowance, which means deducting £1,000 from your gross income and ignoring your actual expenses, or
  • Claim your actual expenses, which means doing the standard "gross income minus expenses" sum.

You cannot do both. The calculator above compares the two methods automatically and shows which one leaves you with less taxable profit.

The £3,000 threshold change is announced but NOT in force. The Government announced on 11 March 2025 that it intends to raise the Self Assessment reporting threshold for trading income from £1,000 to £3,000 "within this Parliament", with a new HMRC online reporting tool promised by 2029. As of the 2026/27 tax year, the threshold remains £1,000. We will update this calculator when the change takes effect.

When the allowance does NOT apply

You cannot use the trading allowance if your income comes from a connected party (for example, your own limited company, your employer, or a partnership you're a partner in). It also cannot create a loss: at most it reduces your taxable income to nil. If you have multiple sources of trading or miscellaneous income, the £1,000 covers all of them in total, not each one separately.

How this interacts with Class 4 National Insurance

Class 4 NI is calculated on your taxable profits, which is the figure AFTER you've applied either the trading allowance or your actual expenses. So if claiming the allowance gets you to a lower taxable profit than claiming expenses, you also save on Class 4 NI as well as Income Tax.
